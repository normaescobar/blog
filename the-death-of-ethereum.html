The death of Ethereum

Ethereum will collapse. The death spiral caused by an engineering flaw boiling up since the proof-of-stake transition. The security of Ethereum is now based on the price of ether and on the faith of the developers. In other words, the growth in market cap of Ethereum, must be followed by a linear growth in the amount "staked" to secure the network.

Currently, $20 billion dollars are staked to protect a market cap of $146 billion (December 20, 2022). You cannot withdraw your staked ETH because the code has not been written. Let's assume the Ethereum developers will write the code to enable you to withdraw your staked ETH by March 2023. Scenario A: It's March 2023. Crypto contagion continues and the price of ETH falls to $600. People unstake their ETH frantically and sell. Creating more downwards pressure on the price, causing more people to unstake, thus making the network security extremely vulnerable.

Scenario B: Ethereum developers do not write the code. Your ETH is now hostage. The security of the network is based on the faith of the developers. Think of what happened after every other project (Celsius) or bank (bank runs) "froze withdrawals?" IMO, both scenarios are plausible.

Scenario C: Ethereum continues to grow and it reaches $100 trillion dollar market cap. It would require $13.5 trillion to secure the network, under the same staking/market cap ratio. Security costs for proof of stake scale linearly with the amount of value being secured. This is a huge problem no one really talks about. There is a huge opportunity cost for the world to have the equivalent of a developed nation's GDP tied up in a staking contract. I can argue this is an issue for all proof-of-stake protocols.

The price of Bitcoin has gone nowhere since 2017. And yet the security of the network (hash rate) has gone from 14 million TH/s to 216 million TH/s. The bitcoin network does not care if the price of bitcoin is $1 or $1,000,000. The difficulty adjustment was coded into the protocol to not rely on the faith of developers or price. The more I think about it, proof-of-work might go down as the most underrated human invention.
